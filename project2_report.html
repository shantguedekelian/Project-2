Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Shant Guedekelian, Jaden Vo, Marcel — sguedekelian@ucsd.edu | j9vo@ucsd.edu | marcel@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Carbon Dioxide and Greenhouse Gas emissions have followed the exact same trend over the years.

FOR the Proposition

Score: 1.5

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Data transformations: The original data was reported quarterly (e.g., “2015Q1”, “2015Q2”). We removed the quarter labels and kept only the year. We then grouped the data by year and calculated the mean, minimum, and maximum values across the four quarters. Using yearly averages reduces short-term fluctuations, making the final plot smoother and easier to interpret.
  • Axis manipulation: The y-axis scales for greenhouse gases and carbon dioxide were adjusted independently, causing the line plots to look similar. Since we argue that the greenhouse gases and carbon dioxide follow the same trend, the magnitudes do not matter as long as we see that the trends are similar in both graphs. The values are indeed similar in trend, but adjusting the axis increments makes it even more convincing as the resulting lines are incredibly similar.
  • Gestalt Principle, figure: We plotted the range of min and max values which appear as the shadow of the graph, adding an additional layer of uncertainty to the graph. The viewer's eyes must now focus on 3 separate trends, the minimum, maximum, and the average line trends. This increases visual complexity, which obscures direct comparison and adds uncertainty to each graph.
  • Gestalt Principle, proximity: Separating the two graphs makes it much harder to compare the trends of the lines at the same time. This separation reduces direct comparability, making differences less noticeable. As a result, the viewer perceives the two trends as more similar and smoother than they might actually be.
